What to get tested — and why

Once-a-year labs to build a baseline you can track over time. Codes included are for Labcorp, covered by most insurance with the correct billing codes (ICD-10).

At your doctor visit, explain that "you're looking to establish a 'normal baseline' that you can track overtime." The idea is you want to know which biomarkers are optimal, not just "normal".

Some docs may push back (ego-dependent) but ideally your doctor is open to working with you and your goals. If not, might be worth trying another doctor who aligns with your values and is preventing focused. Some might also say "if you're asymptomatic you shouldn't test", but that is the point; you want a normal baseline for yourself. If you do have symptoms that's even more of a reason to test.

Framing matters though. Instead of demanding these tests "just because", state your goals clearly. Your goal might be longevity, and you want to focus on preventative care to spot early warning signs, if anything comes up abnormal. (ie. seeing a downward trend over time that never get flagged cause they're within normal ranges).

If they are still questioning this, you can say you have a friend who is a 'health nerd' who put this together for you lol.

On insurance - they will probably tell you some of these won't be covered by insurance. There's a possibility that's true, but that really comes down to the doctor putting in the correct ICD-10 code and some doctors just might not feel comfortable doing that if they feel like they're using codes even though you're asymptomatic. However all preventative minded physicians believe it's just as warranted, so it just comes down to what the doctor is comfortable with.

If this comes up, you can say something like "there are example ICD-10 codes already there that we can try, but even if I have to pay for some of these I would still want to give it a try."

Personally, I've done these labs multiple times a year for the last four years and haven't paid anything, but just know that there is a chance some of these may not be covered and mileage may vary. But I think at the end of the day it's worth the cost to have your data.

Ask for labs to check that everything is optimal, not just normal — that word matters. Day 1 requires a 12-hour fast. Day 2 does not. Hydrate well.

Day 1 — Fasting
#LabCodeWhy it matters
1Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)322000Screens kidney function, liver enzymes, electrolytes, and blood sugar in one draw. Your metabolic foundation.
2CBC with Differential005009Checks red and white blood cells and platelets. Flags anemia, infection, and immune abnormalities.
3Urinalysis, Complete with Microscopic003772Kidney and bladder health, plus early metabolic issues that show up in urine before blood markers shift.
4NMR LipoProfile with Lipids884247Goes beyond total cholesterol to measure actual particle count and size — a far better cardiovascular risk predictor.
5Apolipoprotein B167015The most accurate single marker for cardiovascular risk. Each ApoB particle can deposit in artery walls.
6Lipoprotein(a) Test once120188A genetically determined cardiovascular risk factor. Only need to measure it once — it rarely changes.
7Hemoglobin A1c001453Your 3-month average blood sugar. More reliable than a fasting glucose snapshot.
8Insulin, Fasting004333Elevated fasting insulin is one of the earliest signs of metabolic dysfunction — often rises years before glucose does.
9C-Reactive Protein, Cardiac (hs-CRP)120766Measures systemic inflammation. Elevated levels predict cardiovascular events better than cholesterol alone.
10Iron and Total Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC)001321Iron deficiency is the most common nutrient deficiency worldwide. TIBC gives context to your iron level.
11Ferritin004598Your iron storage marker. Both very low and very high ferritin are problematic and often missed on standard panels.
12Phosphorus001024Involved in bone health, energy production, and kidney function. Often depleted in processed food-heavy diets.

View:

Hormone levels shift throughout your cycle, so timing is key. Day 1 (Cycle Day 3): Book your draw on days 2, 3, or 4 of your cycle. Day 2 (Cycle Day 19–21): Count 7 days back from your expected next period — Day 21 for a 28-day cycle, Day 23 for a 30-day cycle. Fast 10–12 hours for both draws. Book the same cycle days each year for consistent comparisons.
